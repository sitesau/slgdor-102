Signs your garage door spring needs replacing

Several symptoms signal that a garage door spring is nearing or has reached the end of its service life. A loud bang from inside the garage is the most obvious sign of a torsion spring snapping. The door may appear uneven when partially open, with one side higher than the other. The opener motor may struggle, stall or refuse to lift the door at all. A door that falls or drops quickly when released from the motor indicates a complete loss of spring tension.

Gradual symptoms include a door that feels noticeably heavier when lifted manually, a visible gap in the torsion spring coil, or the motor making unusual straining noises during operation. A spring that has lost significant tension may still lift the door partially but will cause accelerated wear on the opener motor. The specialist confirms the diagnosis on site by measuring the spring, checking the gap in the coil for torsion types, and testing the balance of the door with the motor disengaged.

Torsion springs versus extension springs

Torsion springs are the standard on most modern sectional garage doors across Ipswich. They mount horizontally on a steel shaft above the door, with the spring wound under high tension. When the door closes, the spring stores energy. When the door opens, the spring releases that energy to assist the lift. Torsion springs are generally rated for a higher cycle life and provide smoother, more controlled door movement.

Extension springs are more common on older installations and lighter single-width doors. They stretch along the horizontal track on each side, with a safety cable running through the centre to catch the spring if it breaks. Extension spring replacement is typically less expensive per spring, but torsion springs offer longer service life and safer operation. The specialist identifies the spring type, measures the wire gauge, inside diameter and length, and sources a matching replacement rated to the specific door weight. Brands common to the corridor include B&D, Steel-Line, Gliderol and others, so replacement springs match the existing door specification.

Why spring replacement is not a DIY job

Garage door springs are under extreme tension. A torsion spring on a standard residential door holds enough stored energy to cause serious injury or death if it releases uncontrollably. Winding and unwinding torsion springs requires specialised winding bars of the correct diameter, applied to the spring cone in a precise sequence. Extension springs, while less dangerous, still carry significant tension and can cause lacerations or impact injuries if they release while being handled.

QBCC-licensed specialists carry the correct tools, safety equipment and replacement springs rated to the door specification. They follow a controlled winding procedure that releases tension incrementally, preventing the sudden release of stored energy. The specialist also checks the shaft, bearings, cables and drums for wear that could cause the new spring to fail prematurely. Common questions

How long do garage door springs last? Garage door spring life depends on the spring cycle rating, usage frequency and door weight. Most residential torsion springs are rated for 10,000 to 15,000 cycles, which translates to roughly 7 to 12 years of typical family use. Heavy doors and frequent operation reduce service life.

Should both springs be replaced if only one breaks? When a door has two torsion springs and one fails, replacing both is generally recommended. The second spring has experienced the same cycle count and is likely near failure. Replacing both at the same time restores balanced lifting and avoids a second callout fee in the near future.

Can I replace a garage door spring myself? No. Garage door torsion springs are under extreme tension and can cause serious injury or death if handled incorrectly. Spring replacement requires specialised winding bars and a controlled tension-release procedure performed by a QBCC-licensed specialist with the correct tools and safety equipment.
